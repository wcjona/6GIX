SYNTHETIC BIOLOGY SOLUTION TO EUTROPHICATION DETECT

WET LAB OVERVIEW

Problem

Eutrophication is a highly detrimental environmental condition that results from the flow of excess nutrients into aquatic systems (Carpenter 2005). This subsequently promotes the growth of plants and algal which results in depleted oxygen stores and the death of many aquatic organisms (Carpenter 2005). Chlorophyll a is often used as an indicator of water quality since it is able to indicate eutrophication in lakes (Doering et al., 2006). Since chlorophyll a is a photosynthetic pigment, it can be used as a direct indicator of algal production and ultimately eutrophic conditions if high concentrations of chlorophyll a are detected (Gholizadeh et al., 2016). Thus, detecting chlorophyll a levels will serve as an invaluable diagnostic tool to determine the level of water pollution in aquatic systems, so further steps can be taken to reduce the pollution. Currently, spectrometers with 90% acetone are used to quantify the level of chlorophyll a in water systems (Johan et al., 2014). However, this detection method is expensive, difficult to use, and slow, which makes it undesirable(Johan et al., 2014, Johan et al. 2018), and signifies the need for a novel solution.

Solution

Our proposed solution is 6GIX. 6GIX is a water-soluble chlorophyll-binding protein (WSCP), composed of 4 subunits (Palm et al., 2018), which is hypothesized to act as a transient carrier of chlorophyll a (Sillner et al., 2019). Due to the hydrophobic nature of chlorophyll a, emulsions can be used to capture and bind chlorophyll a to 6GIX (Sillner et al., 2019). Although 6GIX normally has a higher affinity for chlorophyll b, when a 4-6 amino acid loop is added, 6GIX will have a higher affinity to chlorophyll a (Palm et al., 2018). Thus, the gene encoding for 6GIX can be engineered and expressed in E. Coli, which allows for mass protein production (iGEM Calgary Team, 2019). This can then be used as a chlorophyll detection system, enabling the quality of water to be determined.

PROTOCOL




1. Digestion:

1) For insert DNA, to a microcentrifuge tube, add:
    - 15 ul of DNA
    - 2μl of 10x Buffer
    - 1μl of EcoR1
    - 1μl of PstI
    - 1 μl of ddH20
    (Final volume = 20 μl)

2) For vector DNA, to a microcentrifuge tube, add:
    - 5μl of psB1C3
    - 1μl of 2.1 x buffer
    - 1μl of EcoR1
    - 1μl of PstI
    - 2μl of ddH20
    (Final volume = 10 μl)

3) Incubate at 37°C for 30 minutes to 3 hours

4) Heat inactivate restriction enzymes at 82°C for 20 minutes

5) For digest confirmations, run on regular agarose gel. For subsequent ligation, run on LMP gel and excise or gel-extract. Otherwise, store at -20°C

2. Ligation:

1) To a microcentrifuge tube, add:
    - 2 ul of Digested vector DNA
    - 20ul of Digested insert DNA (psB1C3)
    - 3ul of aliquoted 10X T4 DNA ligase buffer
    - 1μl of T4 DNA ligase (1U/μl)
    - 4ul of ddH2O
    (Final volume = 30 ul)

2) Incubate at room temperature for 2 hours

3) Transform chemically competent cells with the ligation product.

4) Leave remaining ligation product at room temperature overnight, and transform again the following day.

5) Store remaining product at -20°C




3. Transformation:

1) Add ≤ 5μl (≤ 1/10 of the cell aliquot amount) of the DNA sample to a chemically competent cell aliquot. Mix by pipetting gently, then incubate on ice for 30 to 45 minutes.

2) Heat shock at 42°C for 60-75s minutes

3) Incubate on ice for 5 minutes

4) Add 250μl of plain LB or SOC media aseptically, then incubate for 30 to 90 minutes at 37°C, shaking
    - If resistance is Kan, must incubate for at least 1 hour
    - SOC media = more nutrient rich media that may help growth

5) Plate 100μl (if big plates) or 50μl (if small or half plates) aseptically

6) Incubate plate at 37°C overnight or until growth is observed
     NOTE: Usually colonies that appear after two days are false positives

4. cPCR:

1) Create cPCR mastermix as follows:
    - 20μL 10X Taq Buffer
    - 4μL 10μM VF2 primer (or other forward primer)
    - 4μL 10μM VR primer (or other reverse primer)
    - 4μL 10mM dNTPs
    - 1μL Taq Polymerase
    - 127μL ddH2O

2) Add 4μL of ddH2O to each PCR tube or well (of a 96-well plate)

3) Using pipette tips or sterile toothpicks, touch an individual labelled colony, swirl in the ddH2O, and then streak on masterplate. Repeat for as many colonies as desired.

4) Add 16μL of cPCR mastermix to each tube/well

5) Place in thermocycler and select or set up the appropriate program as follows:
    Initial denaturation → 95°C for 5 minutes
    Repeat 30x:
        i. Denaturation → 95°C for 30 seconds
        ii. Annealing → Tm - 5°C (For VF2/VR primers, 53°C) for 30 seconds
         iii. Extension → 68°C for 1 minute per kilobase
    - Final extension → 68°C for 5 minutes
    - Hold at 4°C




5. Gel Electrophoresis:

1) Mix 100ml of 1X TAE buffer with 1g of regular or LMP agarose (for a 1% gel). For a small gel, mix 30ml of 1X TAE with 0.3g agarose.

2) Microwave covered for about 1.5 minutes or until agarose is dissolved

3) Cool and add 4μl SYBR safe. For a small gel, add 1.5μl SYBR safe.

4) Set up gel tray and balance properly. Pour and cast gel. LMP gels should be cast in the fridge.

5) Run sample at 100V (regular gel) or 80V (LMP gel) until sample is ¾ of the way down the gel (20-30min)

Next Steps:

We have currently accomplished a successful digestion, ligation, transformation, colony PCR, and gel electrophoresis. The next steps are to conduct a plasmid miniprep for digest confirmation and transform 6GIX into BL21 (DE3) E. coli. This is followed by subculturing and protein induction, before protein extraction and purification. Finally we will conduct an SDS page and Western Blot, which is followed by the characterization of our protein.
