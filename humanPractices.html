Human Practices

The Problem


Eutrophication is the over-enrichment of nutrients in aquatic ecosystems leading to excessive plant and algal growth. Eutrophication is a highly detrimental environmental condition and is a leading cause of impairment in many freshwater and coastal marine ecosystems globally. While eutrophication can occur naturally over centuries, human activities have accelerated the rate and extent of eutrophication. With the estimated cost of damage by eutrophication in the U.S. being approximately $2.2 billion annually(Dodds et al. 2009), it is important to detect signs of eutrophication early on in order to prevent further damage to the whatever.

Ideate

We propose the use of a chlorophyll-binding protein used in an emulsion system to remove the chlorophyll from canola oil pressed from green seeds. To verify the need of this solution we would like to speak to a representative from the Canadian Canola Commision.

Chlorophyll a is often used as an indicator of water quality since it is a photosynthetic pigment that can be used as a direct indication of algal production thus indicating eutrophication in lakes (Doering et al., 2006). However, the current detection methods are expensive, difficult to use, and slow, which is undesirable. Our proposed solution is 6GIX, a water-soluble chlorophyll-binding protein (WSCP), which is hypothesized to act as a transient carrier of chlorophyll a (Sillner et al., 2019). Due to the hydrophobic nature of chlorophyll a, emulsions can be used to capture and bind chlorophyll a to 6GIX (Sillner et al., 2019). The gene encoding for 6GIX can be engineered and expressed in E. Coli, which allows for mass protein production (iGEM Calgary Team, 2019). This can then be used as a chlorophyll detection system, enabling the quality of water to be determined.
